Department Overview

The Content Department produces all written assets that drive organic rankings and convert visitors into patients. Content writers work closely with the On-Page SEO team — who supply keyword research and content briefs — and produce five content types: blog posts, service pages, location pages, content refreshes, and meta titles and descriptions. Each writer handles up to 30 clients, ensuring enough time to produce high-quality, medically accurate, E-E-A-T aligned content.

Content Type Deliverables

Each content type has its own production process, quality standards, and word count targets.

✍️

Blog Posts

1 per client per month — informational, condition, or service-based

  • Receive content brief from On-Page SEO team: target keyword, intent, outline, internal links
  • Research topic thoroughly — especially for healthcare clients where accuracy is non-negotiable
  • Write to target word count (800–1,500 words depending on topic competitiveness)
  • Use primary keyword in H1, first 100 words, at least one H2, and naturally throughout body
  • Include at least 2 internal links to relevant service or location pages
  • Add a clear CTA at the end (e.g. "Book a consultation today")
  • Format with scannable H2/H3 subheadings, short paragraphs, and bullet points where appropriate
  • Deliver in Google Doc with suggested meta title and meta description
🏥

New Service Pages

Targeting high-intent commercial keywords

  • Receive brief from On-Page SEO: primary keyword, competitor pages, word count target
  • Write to 600–1,200 words — longer if competitive analysis shows top-ranking pages are more in-depth
  • Lead with the service name and primary keyword in the H1
  • Include: what the service is, who it's for, what to expect, why choose this practice
  • Weave in secondary keywords (e.g. "dental implants Toronto") naturally
  • Include FAQ section where appropriate, targeting People Also Ask questions
  • Add schema-ready FAQ formatting — clearly marked for the On-Page team to add FAQPage schema
  • End with a strong CTA and booking prompt
📍

Location Pages

Geo-targeted pages for local search visibility

  • Receive brief from On-Page SEO: city/neighbourhood target, primary keyword, nearby landmarks or context
  • Write to 400–700 words — enough for substance without thin-content risk
  • H1 must include the service + location (e.g. "Dental Implants in Mississauga")
  • Reference the specific neighbourhood, nearby landmarks, or local context naturally
  • Do not copy-paste from other location pages — each page must be meaningfully unique
  • Include the practice's NAP details within the copy or structured section
  • Add a CTA with a local angle (e.g. "Serving patients in Mississauga and surrounding areas")
  • Flag to On-Page team if LocalBusiness schema and Google Map embed are needed
🔄

Content Refreshes

Reviving underperforming or outdated pages

  • Receive refresh brief from On-Page SEO: current URL, reason for refresh (outdated, thin, keyword drift), and updated target keyword
  • Review the existing page and GSC data before writing — understand what's already ranking
  • Retain any sections that are performing; rewrite or expand underperforming sections
  • Update any outdated statistics, treatment information, or references
  • Strengthen internal linking based on the current site structure
  • Ensure updated keyword placement matches current On-Page SEO brief
  • Do not change the URL slug — flag any redirect needs to the On-Page team
  • Mark all changes clearly in the Google Doc using tracked changes or comments
🏷️

Meta Titles & Descriptions

Every new page produced gets optimized meta data — and existing pages are audited on request

  • Meta title: include primary keyword, keep under 60 characters, make it compelling for CTR
  • Meta description: 140–160 characters, summarize the page value, include a soft CTA
  • Never duplicate meta titles or descriptions across pages — each must be unique
  • For service pages: lead meta title with the keyword (e.g. "Root Canal Toronto | [Practice Name]")
  • For blog posts: balance keyword inclusion with readability — avoid robotic phrasing
  • For location pages: always include city name in both meta title and description
  • Flag any existing pages with missing or duplicate meta data to the On-Page SEO team
  • Deliver meta data in the same Google Doc as the content, clearly labelled at the top

Content Quality Standards

E-E-A-T Requirements

  • All medical or health claims must be factually accurate and citable
  • Reference authoritative sources where relevant (e.g. Canadian Dental Association, Health Canada)
  • Avoid making absolute claims about outcomes (e.g. "guaranteed results")
  • Write from the perspective of a knowledgeable, trustworthy practitioner
  • Do not fabricate patient experiences, statistics, or testimonials
  • For YMYL (Your Money Your Life) content, extra care is required — flag any uncertainty to the team lead

SEO Writing Rules

  • One H1 per page — must include the primary keyword
  • Primary keyword used in first 100 words naturally
  • No keyword stuffing — keyword density should feel natural, not forced
  • Use semantic variations and related terms throughout (LSI keywords)
  • Every page needs at least 2 internal links — use descriptive anchor text
  • Short paragraphs (2–4 sentences max) for readability on mobile

Formatting Standards

  • Deliver all content in Google Docs — shared with the On-Page SEO team
  • Use Heading styles (H1, H2, H3) — never just bold text as fake headings
  • Include meta title and meta description at the top of every Google Doc
  • Use comments in Google Docs to flag any uncertainties or questions for review
  • Do not include images in drafts — the On-Page team handles media
  • Use tracked changes when revising existing content refreshes

Tone & Voice

  • Warm, professional, and reassuring — especially for healthcare content
  • Write for the patient first, search engine second
  • Avoid jargon without explanation — define medical terms when first introduced
  • Use second person ("you" / "your") to speak directly to the reader
  • CTAs should feel natural and helpful, not pushy or salesy
  • Match the tone to the client — a medical spa differs from a dental clinic

AI Usage Policy

AI writing tools (primarily ChatGPT) are permitted and encouraged for efficiency — but with strict guardrails to ensure quality and accuracy.

✅ Permitted uses

Where AI helps

  • Generating a first draft from the content brief
  • Suggesting H2/H3 subheading structures
  • Drafting FAQ sections based on common patient questions
  • Rewording sentences for clarity or tone
  • Generating meta title and description options to choose from
  • Summarizing competitor content for research purposes
⚠️ Not permitted

Where human judgment is required

  • Publishing AI output without human review and editing
  • Accepting AI-generated medical claims without fact-checking
  • Using AI to fabricate reviews, testimonials, or case studies
  • Submitting content that reads as generic or template-like
  • Ignoring the content brief in favour of AI's own keyword choices
  • Using AI to write content that could mislead patients about outcomes

Monthly Workflow

Writers follow a consistent monthly cycle for each assigned client, coordinating closely with the On-Page SEO team.

1
Receive Content Briefs (Week 1)
Collect all content briefs from the On-Page SEO team for the month. Each brief includes the content type, target keyword, intent, suggested outline, word count target, and required internal links. Clarify any unclear briefs before starting.
2
Research & Outline (Week 1)
For each piece, review top-ranking competitor pages using Ahrefs or Google search. Note the structure, depth, and angle they use. Use ChatGPT to help brainstorm the outline if needed, then validate it against the brief before writing.
3
Draft Content (Week 2)
Write first drafts for all assigned content pieces. Use ChatGPT to accelerate drafting but ensure every draft is reviewed, edited for accuracy, and genuinely useful to the reader. Healthcare content must be fact-checked against authoritative sources.
4
Self-Review & SEO Check (Week 2–3)
Before submitting, check every piece against the brief: correct keyword placement, word count, H1/H2 structure, internal links, CTA, and meta data included at the top of the Doc. Read aloud to catch awkward phrasing or overly robotic AI tone.
5
Submit to On-Page SEO for Review (Week 3)
Share completed Google Docs with the On-Page SEO team. They review for SEO accuracy, keyword placement, and internal linking before approving for upload. Address any feedback within 48 hours.
6
Revisions & Final Approval (Week 3–4)
Implement any revisions requested by the On-Page SEO team. Once approved, the On-Page team handles upload to the CMS. Content writers do not publish directly to client websites. Mark tasks complete in ClickUp / Asana.
